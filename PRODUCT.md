# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Recruiters are the primary audience, with secondary audiences including collaborators, peers, friends, and anyone who wants to learn more about Daniel Xu or reach his other online presences from one place.

Visitors are usually scanning quickly: they need to understand who Daniel is, what he can build, how he thinks, and where to find more. The site should also reward slower exploration through writing, photography, personal details, and expressive interface choices.

## Product Purpose

This site is Daniel Xu's personal home on the web: a portfolio, identity system, link hub, writing archive, photo gallery, and proof of taste. It should make people believe Daniel is creative, technically strong, artistic, and unusually intentional about how he presents himself.

Success means the site feels like evidence, not a claim. The interface itself should demonstrate that Daniel can express personality through code while still making projects, experience, contact paths, and external profiles easy to find.

## Brand Personality

Skeuomorphic, artful, creative.

The tone should feel personal and crafted rather than corporate. It can be quirky, textural, tense, and unconventional, with a sense that the site was made by someone who notices small details and is willing to push past default web patterns.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic developer portfolios, startup SaaS landing pages, gamer or cyberpunk aesthetics, template-like project card grids, and overly sanitized personal branding. The site should not feel like a résumé converted into a webpage, a product marketing funnel, or a dark neon tech demo.

Avoid convention for its own sake, but preserve orientation. Product links, résumé, email, social profiles, projects, writing, and photos must remain discoverable even when the presentation is expressive.

## Design Principles

1. Make the interface behave like evidence of craft: design choices should prove programming ability, taste, and artistic intent without needing explanatory copy.

2. Use skeuomorphism as a source of texture and interaction, not nostalgia alone. Physical metaphors should make the site feel handled, assembled, filed, printed, photographed, marked, or otherwise materially made.

3. Preserve useful tension. Let compositions feel slightly strange, dense, asymmetric, archival, or quirky when that tension creates memorability, but keep the core navigation and contact paths legible.

4. Show range without fragmenting identity. Projects, writing, photography, and résumé details can each have their own mood, but they should still read as parts of one personal system.

5. Prefer memorable specificity over portfolio convention. Replace generic hero claims, identical cards, and polished SaaS rhythm with details that only Daniel's site would contain.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use sane default accessibility requirements: responsive layouts, semantic structure, keyboard-accessible interactions, visible focus states, readable contrast, reduced-motion support, and no meaning conveyed by color alone. Expressive or unconventional treatments should not block scanning, navigation, or contact.
